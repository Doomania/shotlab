What are AI video transitions?

A transition is the visual or editorial technique used to move from one shot to the next. In AI video, the model interprets the transition instruction as a continuity or motion cue — it needs to understand what the outgoing shot looks like, what the incoming shot looks like, and what visual logic connects them. A well-written transition prompt prevents abrupt, random cuts and gives the model enough context to generate a smooth or deliberately jarring edit.

The 5 transition categories

Motion — transitions driven by camera or subject movement: whip pan, smash cut, J-cut, L-cut. Fast, kinetic, high-energy.

Match Cut — transitions built on visual or action rhymes: a shared gesture, an eyeline, a graphic similarity between frames. Creates elegant continuity.

Light — transitions that use exposure or luminosity: fade to black, cross dissolve, light wipe, flash cut. Control emotional tempo.

Space / Time — transitions that reframe location or jump across time: time cut, location cut, split screen, zoom reveal. Useful for narrative jumps.

Texture — transitions that use surface materials or particle effects to wipe between shots: texture wipe, particle burst. Strong visual interest.

Transition prompt formula

Base structure: Shot A: [description]. Transition: [type and technique]. Shot B: [description]. [Style]. [Pacing] pacing, cinematic.

Example: Shot A: warrior raises sword in golden light. Transition: a fast whip pan transition between shots. Shot B: extreme close-up of enemy's eye. Cinematic anime. Steady pacing, cinematic.

Common transition mistakes

  • Not describing Shot A or Shot B — the model has no context for what it is connecting.
  • Using match cut without specifying what the match is — shape, gesture, eyeline, or action.
  • Mixing transition types that contradict each other (e.g. fade to black + whip pan).
  • Forgetting pacing — a slow dissolve and a fast dissolve read very differently.

Transitions vs camera moves

A camera move happens within a shot — dolly in, orbit, crane up. A transition happens between shots — it is editorial, not cinematographic. In one prompt you can combine both: specify the camera move for each shot, then the transition technique between them. FAQ

Can AI video models actually execute transitions?+
It depends on the model and clip length. Seedance and Kling handle intra-clip transitions well. Cross-shot transitions work best when you render two clips separately and use a video editor — but specifying the transition logic in the prompt still conditions the start and end frames of each clip.
Which transition works best for Seedance 9:16 content?+
Whip pan and light wipe perform best in Seedance for vertical content — they're motion-driven and read clearly at mobile scale. Match cuts work well too when the matched element is large and central in frame.
Do I need to specify the transition in both Shot A and Shot B prompts?+
Yes. For two-clip workflows, include the outgoing motion in Shot A's prompt (e.g. "camera whips right, motion blur") and the incoming motion in Shot B's prompt ("camera arrives from left, motion resolves"). The transition builder outputs a combined description you can split across both.
What's the difference between a smash cut and a flash cut?+
A smash cut is an abrupt, jarring edit with no visual transition — pure hard cut used for comedic or shocking contrast. A flash cut inserts a single frame of pure white or overexposed brightness between shots — it softens the impact slightly while still feeling energetic.
